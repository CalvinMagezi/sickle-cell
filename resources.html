Resources /FAQs

  • Are there different types of sickle cell disease?

    Sickle cell is a red blood cell disorder. It is inherited. That means that it is passed down the same way people get the color of their eyes, skin, and hair from their parents, that’s to say, it’s hereditary.

  • How common is it??

    Sickle cell disease contributes substantially to mortality in children younger than 5 years in sub-Saharan Africa. In Uganda, 20 000 babies per year are thought to be born with sickle cell disease, but accurate data are not available.

  • How is it diagnosed??

    A person can have a blood test to find out if he or she has either sickle cell trait or a form of the disease. There is also are prenatal tests to find out if the baby will have the disorder or not.

  • What kind of challenges might patients face?

    Anemia, or low blood levels, occurs in all forms of sickle cell disease. Some children may only have mild anemia, but others will have severe anemia. The anemia can cause a child to feel fatigued or tired, and can affect physical and mental growth as well. The most common and well known complication is pain crisis.
